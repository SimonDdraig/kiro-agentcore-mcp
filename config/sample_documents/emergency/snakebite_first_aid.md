# Snakebite First Aid Procedures

## Emergency Contact
- Emergency services: **000**
- Poisons Information Centre: **13 11 26**

## Identification
Do NOT attempt to catch or kill the snake. Note the colour, size, and
location if possible. A photo from a safe distance can help with identification.
All Australian snakes should be treated as potentially venomous.

## Pressure Immobilisation Technique (PIT)
This is the recommended first aid for all Australian snake bites.

1. Keep the patient calm and still — movement spreads venom through the lymphatic system
2. Call 000 immediately
3. Apply a firm broad pressure bandage over the bite site (do NOT wash the bite — venom residue helps identification)
4. Wrap the bandage firmly from the bite site along the entire limb towards the heart
5. The bandage should be as firm as you would bandage a sprained ankle
6. Splint the limb to prevent movement — use a branch, walking pole, or rolled clothing
7. Mark the bite site on the outside of the bandage with a pen
8. Keep the patient lying down and as still as possible
9. Monitor breathing and be prepared to perform CPR

## Do NOT
- Do NOT cut the bite or attempt to suck out venom
- Do NOT apply a tourniquet
- Do NOT wash the bite site
- Do NOT give the patient alcohol or aspirin
- Do NOT allow the patient to walk if transport is available

## Common Venomous Species in Parks
- Eastern brown snake — fast-moving, highly venomous, found in grasslands
- Tiger snake — wetlands and waterways, neurotoxic venom
- Red-bellied black snake — near water, less aggressive but still dangerous
- Taipan — northern regions, extremely venomous, seek immediate evacuation

## Ranger Notes
Carry a snake bite kit (broad bandages and splint) on all field patrols.
Ensure all staff complete annual first aid refresher including PIT practice.
Record bite location, time, and any symptoms for paramedics.
