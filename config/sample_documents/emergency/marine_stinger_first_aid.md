# Marine Stinger First Aid Procedures

## Emergency Contact
- Emergency services: **000**
- Poisons Information Centre: **13 11 26**

## Box Jellyfish (Chironex fleckeri)
Found in tropical waters of northern Australia from October to May.
A sting from a large box jellyfish is a life-threatening emergency.

### First Aid
1. Call 000 immediately
2. Douse the sting area liberally with vinegar for at least 30 seconds — this deactivates unfired nematocysts
3. Remove any remaining tentacles using tweezers or gloved hands (never bare hands)
4. If the patient is unconscious or not breathing, begin CPR
5. Monitor for cardiac arrest — box jellyfish venom affects the heart
6. Keep the patient still and calm until paramedics arrive

## Irukandji Jellyfish
Tiny (1-2 cm bell) but extremely dangerous. Found in tropical waters.
Symptoms may be delayed 20 to 30 minutes after the sting.

### First Aid
1. Douse with vinegar
2. Seek immediate medical attention — Irukandji syndrome can cause severe pain, hypertension, and cardiac complications
3. Do NOT apply pressure bandage
4. Monitor for symptoms: severe lower back pain, nausea, sweating, anxiety

## Bluebottle (Portuguese Man o' War)
Common on eastern Australian beaches. Painful but rarely dangerous.

### First Aid
1. Remove tentacles with tweezers
2. Immerse the sting area in hot water (as hot as tolerable, not scalding) for 20 minutes
3. Do NOT use vinegar for bluebottle stings — it can worsen the pain
4. Seek medical attention if pain persists or the patient has an allergic reaction

## Ranger Notes
Stinger nets do NOT protect against Irukandji. Ensure stinger season
signage is displayed at all beach access points from October. Carry
vinegar bottles at all coastal patrol stations.
