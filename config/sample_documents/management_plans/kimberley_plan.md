# Kimberley Region Management Plan

## Overview
The Kimberley region in northern Western Australia covers approximately 423,000
square kilometres of remote wilderness. It includes national parks such as
Purnululu (Bungle Bungles), Mitchell River, and Prince Regent. The region
contains some of Australia's most intact ecosystems and is managed by DBCA in
partnership with Aboriginal ranger groups including Uunguu, Balanggarra, and
Dambimangari.

## Conservation Priorities
1. Indigenous ranger-led land and sea management programs
2. Feral cattle and donkey removal from river systems
3. Cane toad front management (advancing westward)
4. Fire management using traditional mosaic burning
5. Protection of Wandjina and Gwion Gwion rock art sites

## Seasonal Considerations
- Wet season (Nov-Apr): Roads impassable, aerial patrols only, crocodile breeding
- Dry season (May-Oct): Ground access restored, controlled burns, tourism season
- Build-up (Sep-Nov): Extreme heat and humidity, storm activity begins

## Key Species
- Gouldian finch (Chloebia gouldiae) — Endangered
- Northern quoll (Dasyurus hallucatus) — Endangered, cane toad vulnerable
- Freshwater crocodile (Crocodylus johnstoni)
- Black-footed tree-rat (Mesembriomys gouldii) — Vulnerable

## Key Threats
- Cane toad invasion devastating native predator populations
- Feral cattle and donkeys degrading riparian zones
- Unmanaged wildfire destroying monsoon vine thickets
- Illegal access to sacred cultural sites
